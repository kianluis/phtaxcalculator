Taxes for Filipino Freelancers & Virtual Assistants

A plain-language walkthrough of every tax obligation you have as a self-employed professional in the Philippines — what to file, when to file it, and how much to pay.

Overview

If you earn income as a freelancer, virtual assistant, online worker, or independent contractor in the Philippines, you are considered self-employed under the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR). This means you are responsible for computing and paying your own taxes — your clients do not withhold anything on your behalf (unless you are hired locally).

As a self-employed individual, you are generally subject to two types of taxes:

  1. Income Tax — paid quarterly and annually (BIR Forms 1701Q and 1701)
  2. Percentage Tax — paid quarterly if your gross receipts are below ₱3 million (BIR Form 2551Q), unless you opted for the 8% flat rate
Good news: If you choose the 8% income tax rate, the percentage tax is automatically replaced. You only file one type of tax instead of two.

BIR Registration

Before you can legally operate as a freelancer in the Philippines, you must register with the BIR. Here's how:

  1. Get a TIN (Tax Identification Number) — If you've been employed before, you already have one. Never get a second TIN; it's illegal.
  2. Register as self-employed — File BIR Form 1901 at the Revenue District Office (RDO) that has jurisdiction over your home address.
  3. Choose your tax regime — Indicate whether you want the 8% flat rate or the graduated rate on Form 1901 (or on your first 1701Q of the year).
  4. Register your books of accounts — You are required to maintain records of your income and expenses. A simple cashbook or spreadsheet is acceptable for most freelancers.
  5. Get an Authority to Print (ATP) or register official receipts — You need to issue Official Receipts (OR) to your clients for all payments received.
Note: Many online freelancers skip BIR registration. This is technically illegal and can result in penalties. Registration is simple and costs very little.

The 8% Income Tax Rate

Introduced by the TRAIN Law (Republic Act 10963) in 2018, the 8% option is designed to simplify tax compliance for self-employed individuals.

How it works

You pay 8% of your gross income or receipts (minus the first ₱250,000, which is exempt). This replaces both income tax and percentage tax.

Annual Tax = (Gross Annual Income − ₱250,000) × 8%

Who can use it?

  • Self-employed individuals and professionals (freelancers, VAs, consultants, etc.)
  • Your annual gross income must not exceed ₱3,000,000
  • You must not be VAT-registered
  • You must not derive income from mixed employment and self-employment at the same time (unless your employer already excludes compensation from the election)

Key benefits

  • No need to track business expenses for deductions
  • Replaces the 3% percentage tax — fewer forms to file
  • Simple and predictable — good for budgeting

How to elect the 8% rate

Indicate your election on your first quarterly return (1701Q) for the year, or on BIR Form 1901 at registration. Once elected, it applies for the entire calendar year.

Important: If your income exceeds ₱3,000,000 during the year, you must switch to the graduated rate for that year and pay percentage tax starting from the quarter when you exceeded the threshold.

The Graduated Income Tax Rate

This is the traditional tax system where your tax rate increases as your income grows. It uses a bracket system applied to your net taxable income (after deductions).

What counts as "net taxable income"?

Net taxable income = Gross Income − Allowable Deductions. Self-employed individuals can choose between:

  • Itemized deductions — Deduct your actual business expenses with receipts and documentation
  • Optional Standard Deduction (OSD) — Deduct a flat 40% of gross income automatically, no receipts required

Most freelancers and VAs use OSD because it's simpler and often results in a lower tax than itemizing.

Note: Under the graduated rate, you are still required to file and pay the 3% Percentage Tax (Form 2551Q) separately each quarter — unless you are VAT-registered.

Optional Standard Deduction (OSD)

OSD is the simplest way to reduce your taxable income under the graduated rate. You deduct 40% of your gross receipts or gross income — no itemization, no receipts, no documentation required.

Net Taxable Income = Gross Annual Income × (1 − 40%) = Gross × 60%

For example, if you earn ₱600,000/year:

  • OSD deduction = ₱600,000 × 40% = ₱240,000
  • Net taxable income = ₱600,000 − ₱240,000 = ₱360,000
  • Tax on ₱360,000 = ₱0 (first ₱250,000) + (₱360,000 − ₱250,000) × 15% = ₱16,500

How to elect OSD

Indicate your election on your first quarterly return (1701Q) for the year. Once elected, it applies for the entire calendar year and cannot be changed to itemized deductions mid-year.

Quarterly Tax Filing — BIR Form 1701Q

Self-employed individuals must file quarterly income tax returns (Form 1701Q) for the first three quarters of the year. These are advance payments credited against your annual tax due.

Cumulative method

Quarterly filings use a cumulative method: each quarter you report total income from January to date, compute the total tax, then subtract what you've already paid in previous quarters. This means:

  • Q1: Pay tax on income from Jan–Mar only
  • Q2: Pay tax on Jan–Jun income, minus what you paid in Q1
  • Q3: Pay tax on Jan–Sep income, minus Q1 + Q2 payments
  • Annual: Pay the remaining balance (or get a refund if you overpaid)

If your income is steady throughout the year, the 8% exemption (₱250,000) may cause your Q1 payment to be ₱0, with payments increasing in Q2 and Q3.

Annual Income Tax Return — BIR Form 1701

At the end of each tax year, you file Form 1701 to reconcile your total income and tax for the full year. All quarterly payments made throughout the year are credited here.

Deadline

April 15 of the following year (e.g., April 15, 2025 for tax year 2024).

What happens at filing

  • If your quarterly payments exceed your actual annual tax — you get a refund or tax credit
  • If your quarterly payments are less than your actual annual tax — you pay the balance due

Form 1701 vs 1701A

  • Form 1701 — For taxpayers who have mixed income (employment + self-employment) or are using itemized deductions
  • Form 1701A — Simplified form for purely self-employed individuals using OSD or the 8% rate

Most freelancers with no day job will use Form 1701A.

Percentage Tax — BIR Form 2551Q

If you are under the graduated rate and your annual gross income is below ₱3,000,000, you must also pay a 3% percentage tax on your gross receipts each quarter.

Quarterly Percentage Tax = Gross Receipts for the Quarter × 3%

Filing deadlines

Form 2551Q is due on the 25th day of the month following the end of each quarter:

  • Q1 (Jan–Mar): Due April 25
  • Q2 (Apr–Jun): Due July 25
  • Q3 (Jul–Sep): Due October 25
  • Q4 (Oct–Dec): Due January 25 (next year)
If you elected the 8% rate: You do NOT file Form 2551Q. The 8% rate already replaces the percentage tax. This saves you one filing per quarter.

Example: Filing 90 days late with ₱10,000 tax due

  • Surcharge: ₱10,000 × 25% = ₱2,500
  • Interest: ₱10,000 × 12% × (90 ÷ 365) = ₱295.89
  • Compromise: ₱1,000
  • Total: ₱13,795.89 (38% more than the original tax)
Don't wait. The longer you delay, the more the interest grows. File immediately even if you can't pay the full amount — at least the surcharge stops applying to future months.

Which rate is better for you?

The right choice depends on your income level and whether you have significant business expenses.

8% is usually better when:

  • Your annual income is between ₱250,000 and ~₱720,000
  • You have low or no business expenses to deduct
  • You want simplicity and fewer filings
  • You earn entirely through foreign clients (USD, AUD, EUR payments)

Graduated + OSD is usually better when:

  • Your annual income is below ₱250,000 (you'd pay 0% — but you still must file)
  • Your income is in the mid-to-high range and the bracket math results in a lower effective rate than 8%
  • You have mixed income with an employer (the 8% election rules become more complex)

The crossover point

At roughly ₱720,000 – ₱750,000 in annual gross income, the two rates tend to produce a similar tax amount. Below this, graduated + OSD is often cheaper. Above this, the 8% flat rate may be better — but use the calculator above to check your exact figures, as the crossover shifts based on which brackets your net income falls into.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I really need to pay taxes if I'm just a VA and my client is abroad?

Yes. Philippine income tax applies to all income earned by Philippine residents, regardless of the client's location or the currency of payment. If you are residing in the Philippines, your freelance earnings are taxable here.

I've never registered. Is it too late?

No. You can register with the BIR at any time. You will need to pay any back taxes and penalties for unreported income, but voluntary disclosure is always treated better than being caught in an audit. Many freelancers use a CPA to help regularize their standing.

What if I earn below ₱250,000 a year?

Under the graduated rate, your income tax is ₱0. Under the 8% rate, you also pay ₱0 (since the first ₱250,000 is exempt). However, you are still required to register with the BIR and file your returns — just with no tax due. You may still owe percentage tax (3%) under the graduated rate.

My income varies month to month. What should I enter in the calculator?

Enter your best estimate of your average monthly income. For an accurate annual picture, use your total expected annual income divided by 12. If your income fluctuates significantly, consider running the calculation at both your highest and lowest expected income to see the range.

Can I switch between 8% and graduated every year?

Yes. Your election applies for one tax year. You can choose a different rate when you file your first 1701Q for the following year. This lets you pick whichever rate is more beneficial for your projected income each year.

What is eFPS and eBIRForms?

eFPS (Electronic Filing and Payment System) is the BIR's online portal for large taxpayers. eBIRForms is the software package available to all taxpayers that allows you to prepare and submit tax returns electronically. Most freelancers use eBIRForms.

Do I need to issue official receipts to foreign clients?

Technically yes, you are required to issue official receipts for all income received. However, many freelancers working with foreign clients issue unofficial acknowledgment receipts or invoices. To fully comply, register for official receipts through your RDO after completing BIR registration.

What about SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG?

These are separate from your BIR obligations. As a self-employed individual, you are allowed (and encouraged) to pay voluntary contributions to SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG to maintain your social benefits. Contributions are based on your declared monthly income. This calculator does not cover these — focus on BIR obligations first.
